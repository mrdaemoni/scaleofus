Chapter 1

The Boy Who Tried to Catch the Wind

Alfred Korzybski, Science and Sanity — Korzybski wrote that a map is different from the place it describes. The boy's card works in the same way. It gives true facts, but it cannot hold the whole boy.

Martin Buber's I and Thou asks what changes when we meet someone as a person instead of treating them as an object. That is also the question the boy brings to the machine.

Related essay: The Humorphic Partnership, about making a machine that helps a person become more human.

Chapter 2

The Stonecutter

Heraclitus, Fragments — Heraclitus used a river to show that the world is always changing. We give the river one name even though its water never stays still.

Walter Ong's Orality and Literacy looks at what changes when spoken words become written things. Robin Wall Kimmerer's Braiding Sweetgrass shows how language can help us see the living world with more care.

Chapter 3

The Potter and the Fire

Michael Polanyi, The Tacit Dimension — Polanyi says that we know more than we can explain. The boy can walk without being able to describe each movement. The potter's hands know things that do not fit into a sentence.

Maurice Merleau-Ponty also treats the body as a way of knowing. Iain McGilchrist's The Master and His Emissary looks at the difference between naming separate parts and attending to a whole living thing.

Related essay: The Weather Between Us, about learning to notice what takes time to feel.

Chapter 4

The Girl at the Pond

Zhuangzi — A line in the Zhuangzi says that still water can act as a mirror. The girl learns by becoming quiet enough to notice what is already there.

Gemma Corradi Fiumara's The Other Side of Language treats listening as part of language, not as the empty time before we speak. Max Picard's The World of Silence also asks what silence makes possible.

Related essay: The Weather Between Us, about attention, slowness, and the space between two people.

Chapter 5

The House of Mirrors

Ovid, Metamorphoses — The story of Narcissus is a warning about confusing a reflection with a relationship. A mirror cannot answer in its own voice.

Byung-Chul Han's The Expulsion of the Other asks what happens when we remove people and ideas that do not agree with us. Hartmut Rosa's Resonance describes a living relationship as one in which the other side can answer and change us.

Related essay: The Humorphic Partnership, about building a machine that can disagree, surprise, and still remain close.

Chapter 6

The Top of the Mountain

Breath, wind, and spirit — Many old languages connect these ideas. The boy reaches the top and finds that the wind is not an object he can hold. It is also the air moving through him.

David Abram's The Spell of the Sensuous looks at air as the shared space where speech and life happen. The Cloud of Unknowing is about meeting what thought cannot fully grasp.

Related essay: The Weather Between Us, about staying with an experience before trying to turn it into an answer.

Chapter 7

One Turn Higher

T. S. Eliot, Four Quartets — Eliot writes about returning to a familiar place and seeing it differently. The boy comes home with the same card, but he is no longer the same reader.

Antonio Machado writes that the path is made by walking. The old Ten Ox-Herding Pictures also end back in ordinary life. Andy Clark and David Chalmers's “The Extended Mind” asks how tools outside the body can become part of how we remember and think.

Related essay: The Humorphic Partnership, about what can happen when a machine gives your own words back to you in a new way.

The talking machine in the story comes from a real project. I built Alicia to read my writing and help me notice what I might miss. You can read the essays behind this story at At the Human Scale.
