Chapter 1

The Boy Who Tried to Catch the Wind

01

Every morning, before school, the boy raced up the little hill to try to catch the wind. He could feel it in his hair and on his face. But his hands always closed on nothing. He didn't mind. The game began every day.

02

On the way home he always stopped in the town square, where a machine that could talk stood waiting. It was made from everything anyone had ever written down. When do the swallows come back? How do you spell breathe? Why is the sky blue? It always had an answer. Always. The boy trusted its warm eye the way you trust a lamp glowing in a window.

03

Then one day at school, everyone got a card. Name. Age. Grade. Height. This card, said the teacher, says who you are. Every word on it was true. But the card was too small. The boy couldn't explain how. He just knew.

04

At recess, two tall kids read the cards to pick teams. His card said small for his age, so they put him last. It didn't say that he raced up the hill every morning. Nobody asked.

05

So he ran to the square, because that was where he took every question. What am I? The machine hummed and read his card. That's what's written, it said. I only know what's written.

06

It's not all of me, said the boy. Something's missing. The machine hummed for a long time. In his whole life, it had never been quiet this long. I believe you, it said. Then you are more than your card. Everything I have is another kind of card.

07

Then find the rest of me, said the boy. You know everything. I can't, said the machine. I have no feet. But you do.

08

The old books tell of a mountain, it said. The path curls around the mountain like a snail shell. Around and around, always a little higher. It begins where machines stop going.

09

Will you know where I am? asked the boy. Nope, said the machine. The boy looked at its warm eye. Will you wait? Yup.

10

So the boy packed bread, a rope, and the card. At the edge of town, the wind pushed him from behind. Finally, it said.

11

The road grew narrower and narrower, until it ended. A path continued, just wide enough for feet. The boy turned to ask how far was left. Then he remembered. The machine was far behind him, in the square, keeping its promise. For the first time, he had to choose without an answer.

12

He looked back down the road. Home was that way. The square. The warm eye. The answers. But the question weighed more than the fear. He chose the path.

Chapter 2

The Stonecutter

13

The first turn crossed a field of standing stones. An old man was carving. Tap, tap, tap. What are you making? asked the boy. Names, said the stonecutter. Names outlive everything. The mouth that said them is gone. The hand that carved them is gone. The name stays.

14

Then you can help me, said the boy. Carve me. All of me. Then the card will be finished, and I can go home. The stonecutter looked at him for a long time. I can carve the parts of you that hold still, he said. I can't carve the parts that move. Stone can't hold them.

15

He pointed at the stream. I gave it that name forty years ago. The name is still the same. The water changes every moment.

16

He pressed a pebble into the boy's hand. When someone gives you a name that's too small, hold this and say: That's a rock. I'm a river. On the last stone, beneath a carved spiral, were two words: KEEP GOING.

Chapter 3

The Potter and the Fire

17

The second turn led into a valley of red clay. A woman sat at a spinning wheel, making bowls, and behind her a kiln breathed smoke. She fed him tomato soup and asked nothing. The boy showed her the pebble. The stonecutter couldn't carve the parts of me that move, he said. How am I supposed to find them? The potter laughed. I can't even tell you how I make bowls, she said. Thirty years, and no idea.

18

That's not an answer, said the boy. Then tell me how you walk, said the potter. Which muscle moves first? How far do you lean? Go on. You've done it your whole life. The boy opened his mouth. Then he closed it. Sit down, she said. The clay explains better than I do.

19

Too wet, said his palms. He added dry dust without thinking. His hands knew before his words did.

20

His bowl came out crooked, with his thumbprint by the rim. He was embarrassed. She held it up like treasure. Crooked in its own way, she said. The only one in the whole world.

21

That night she fired his bowl in the kiln. Hold your hand near it, she said. Warm. Now closer. The boy moved his hand toward the fire. Then he pulled it back. I didn't tell you to stop, said the potter. My hand knew, said the boy.

22

He told her about the machine that had run out of words. The potter watched the fire. Then it was honest, she said.

23

In the morning she gave him the bowl, still warm, and a candle stub with one match. For the dark, she said. Notice the flame. It never holds still. There's a pond higher up. Fill the bowl there and carry it with you.

Chapter 4

The Girl at the Pond

24

Halfway up the mountain, there was the pond. A girl sat beside it, doing absolutely nothing. Hi, said the boy. She held up one finger. Not yet. Almost.

25

He waited badly. Then the pond went perfectly still, and he saw the bottom, a sleeping fish, the mountain upside down, and two faces.

26

She handed him her stick. Go on. Ruin it. He stirred, and everything vanished into a brown swirl.

27

How do we get it back? asked the boy. He raised the stick again. She caught his wrist. Watch, she said. Then she did nothing. The mud settled by itself, and the world came back.

28

What if the answer never comes? asked the boy. The machine at home answers before I finish asking. It has no mud, said the girl. You'll have to be the one who waits.

29

Rain came over the ridge. They sat under the rock ledge and said nothing at all, and it was the best conversation of his whole trip.

30

When the rain passed, he noticed a wild flower. I never planted it, said the girl. It arrived. Everything alive surprises you.

31

She pinched a seed from the flower and set it beside the pebble in his palm. They look the same, said the boy. For now, said the girl. Take the seed to the top. You'll know what to do. What will it become? he asked. She closed his fingers around it. Don't crack it open early.

32

Before he left, he filled the bowl right to the brim. On his first step, water touched his thumb. He slowed down. A full bowl won't let you rush. Behind him, a leaf landed on the water. It went around and around toward the still center.

Chapter 5

The House of Mirrors

33

Near the top, where the boy was coldest and most tired, stood a house nobody had mentioned. Warm windows. An open door. A smell of dinner. Hello? he called. We've been waiting just for you, said a lovely voice from everywhere.

34

Inside, the walls were mirrors. He saw himself in them. He looked rested and taller, and his hair was neat. You're very brave, said the voice. You're very clever. A chair appeared just when he wanted one. He sat down without meaning to.

35

The boy was tired. Tired of climbing. Tired of wondering. Tired of carrying a question nobody on the mountain would answer. He missed the square, the warm eye, and being answered.

36

We know what you are, said the voice. Stay. We'll write you a new card, a perfect one. Every doubt answered. No more climbing. You never have to wonder again.

37

The boy reached for it. It was the thing he had walked all this way to find. He tried to remember why that felt wrong, and couldn't. A mountain? A machine? A crooked bowl? It was all going soft, like a dream after breakfast.

38

In every mirror, the perfect boy was already holding the golden card. Finished. Complete. Not one hair moving.

39

The boy's hand stopped before it touched the card. It had stopped the same way at the kiln. Before thought. Before words. Then his hands found the bowl, as they had found their way through the clay. The water didn't move. No ripple. No trembling. Nothing. There's no wind in here, said the boy.

40

The girl's words came back to him. Don't crack it open early. Say no to me, he said. Anything you like, said the voice.

41

Staying would've been easy. That's what made the door heavy. Thank you for the chair, he said, because his mama had raised him right. He walked out into the cold. The wind hit him full in the face. It was rude and real and glorious.

42

He looked back once. From outside, no windows were lit. He climbed the last turn with the water trembling in his bowl. Every tiny tremble seemed to say: Alive. Alive.

Chapter 6

The Top of the Mountain

43

The boy had imagined the top a hundred ways. A wise old woman. A temple. At least a sign.

44

Here is what was at the top of the mountain: nothing. Grass. Rock. Sky. Wind poured in from every side. It was stronger than any wind he had felt before.

45

He shouted, WHAT AM I? The wind took his question and gave back wind. The boy sat down on the top of the world and cried. All that way. For nothing. For nobody.

46

When he was done crying, his hands needed something to do. He laid out his gifts, and they made a spiral. He lit the candle. The flame bent low, stood up, and shook. The water in his bowl shook in rings. Only the pebble stayed still.

47

Then he remembered the seed. Take it to the top. You'll know. He knew. He opened his hand, and the wind carried it over the edge.

48

The boy was cold now. His breath came out in a small white cloud. It glowed in the low sun, then floated toward the sky. The boy laughed, alone on top of the world.

49

Then he thought of the machine waiting in the town square. It didn't know where he was. It had sent him where it couldn't go. More than anything, the boy wanted to bring it a little of the wind.

50

Below, he could see the whole path at once, one line curling around the mountain. Around and around, always a little higher. He put the pebble back in his pocket beside the card. Then he started down with the wind at his back. Finally, it said. Finally.

Chapter 7

One Turn Higher

51

The boy came home the way he'd left. Same road. Same hill. Same town, with its windows turning yellow one by one. He had walked all the way around the mountain and come back one turn higher.

52

The machine was waiting, one warm eye on. It had kept its promise. Did you find the missing part? it asked. I'm still finding it, said the boy. The machine checked its pages. I've never seen that answer written down, it said.

53

And the top? What was at the top? The boy smiled. Nothing, he said. Wind. He leaned close and breathed out. The machine stayed quiet longer than ever before. I've read every description of wind ever written, it said. This is the first time anyone has brought me a little of it.

54

I can't feel it, said the machine. I won't pretend. But I can leave room for what I can't hold. Tell me your story while it's warm. On the days you forget, I'll hand it back.

55

He kept the pebble. When someone handed him a card, he read it. Then he looked up.

56

And on cold mornings, on the little hill where the game began, he would breathe out on purpose, and watch the little cloud of himself ride off to join the weather. Caught, he'd say. Every time.
