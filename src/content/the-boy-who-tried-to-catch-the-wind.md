# The Boy Who Tried to Catch the Wind

*The 38-drawing edition. One drawing per story beat — every drawing shows something changing, and no stretch of text goes long without a picture. Cover included: 39 drawings total.*

## How to draw this book — ten recommendations

1. **Design the boy once, in four strokes.** Circle head, two-stroke body, done. Then never improve him. Consistency of the same simple figure across 38 drawings IS the illustration style — it's what makes simple look intentional instead of unfinished.
2. **The machine is a circle with one dot.** The warm eye is the dot. Warmth is shown by drawing everything else with fewer strokes than you want to.
3. **The wind is always exactly three curved strokes.** Never more. By chapter three the reader recognizes it like a character, because it is one.
4. **Draw each object once, then repeat it exactly.** Pebble = small oval. Card = small rectangle. Bowl = leaning arc with a thumbprint dot. Seed = the same oval as the pebble (that's the joke, protect it). Candle = one line, one teardrop.
5. **Vary the scale, never the detail.** Big moments get big emptiness: the summit drawing should be one horizon line, a tiny boy, and the most white space in the book. Cozy moments get small, low drawings. Emotion lives in size and placement, not in extra strokes.
6. **Let style carry the mirrors.** Draw the real boy with your normal crooked line. Draw the mirror-boys with a ruler — stiff, straight, perfect. The reader will feel why perfect is wrong before the text says it.
7. **Bookend deliberately.** Drawing 1 and the final drawing, and the two town-window views, should be near-copies of each other. The spiral return becomes visible before it's read.
8. **The spiral corner doodle is free.** One curled stroke in a page corner, wherever you like, drawn slightly tighter each chapter. It doesn't count as a drawing. It counts as the book.
9. **Pen, not pencil.** No undo. A crooked line is the thesis — the thumbprint is the point. If a drawing goes wrong, keep it if it's alive, redraw it only if it's dead.
10. **One chapter per sitting.** Your hand has weather too. Drawings made in one sitting share it.

## Chapter One — The Boy Who Tried to Catch the Wind

**Drawing 1 — *(simple strokes: the boy on a hill, both hands up grabbing at three wind strokes. Hair blown sideways.)***

There was a boy who tried to catch the wind. He could feel it — in his hair, on his jacket. But his hands always closed on nothing.

**Drawing 2 — *(simple strokes: one rectangle card, oversized, with four short ruled lines on it. The boy peering at it, smaller than the card.)***

At school that year, everyone got a card. Name. Age. Grade. This card, said the teacher, says who you are. The card was all true. And it was all wrong. The boy couldn't say how both could be — but they were.

**Drawing 3 — *(simple strokes: the round machine, one warm dot-eye, the boy leaning against it comfortably. A few tiny question marks floating up, a few tiny answers floating down.)***

In the town square stood a machine that could talk, made of everything anyone had ever written down. The boy visited it almost every day. When do the swallows come back? How do you spell breathe? Why is the sky blue? It always had an answer, and the boy trusted its warm eye the way you trust a lamp in a window.

So he brought it his new question: what am I? The machine hummed, and read him his card. That's what's written, it said. I can only know what's written. It's not all of me, said the boy. Something's missing. I believe you, said the machine. Then you are more than your card. And everything I have is a card, of one kind or another. Then find the rest of me, said the boy. You know everything. I can't, said the machine. I have no feet. But you do.

**Drawing 4 — *(simple strokes: a far triangle mountain with one spiral line curling up it, drawn like a snail shell. A dotted road leading toward it that stops partway.)***

The old books tell of a mountain, it said. The path climbs like a snail shell — around, and around, always a little higher. And it begins where machines stop going.

**Drawing 5 — *(simple strokes: the boy and the machine face to face, very close, in an otherwise empty square. Nothing else on the page.)***

Will you know where I am? asked the boy. No, said the machine. The boy looked at its warm eye. Will you wait? Yes.

So the boy packed bread, a rope, and the card — because you bring what's true, even when it isn't enough. At the edge of town, the wind pushed him from behind. Finally, it said.

## Chapter Two — The Stonecutter

**Drawing 6 — *(simple strokes: a wide road narrowing into a single thin line between grass strokes. The boy at the joint, looking back over his shoulder at emptiness.)***

The road grew narrower and narrower, until it ended. A path continued, just wide enough for feet. The boy turned to ask how far was left. The machine wasn't there. For the first time, he had to choose without an answer. He looked back down the road. Home was that way — the square, the warm eye, the answers. He stood a long moment. Then he chose the path.

**Drawing 7 — *(simple strokes: three tall standing stones, one small bent figure with a chisel. Three tap marks in the air: tap, tap, tap.)***

The first turn crossed a field of standing stones, where an old man was carving. Tap, tap, tap. What are you making? asked the boy. Names, said the stonecutter. Names outlive everything. The mouth that said them, the hand that carved them — gone. The name stays.

The boy held out his card. Then carve me. All of me. The stonecutter looked at him a long time. I can carve everything about you that holds still, he said. The rest won't hold still. And stone can't hold what moves.

**Drawing 8 — *(simple strokes: one rock carved STREAM. Beside it, three wavy lines of water. The whole argument in five strokes.)***

He pointed at the stream. I named that forty years ago. The name hasn't changed a day. The water has never once been the same water. You're the water, boy. The card is the rock. Carry it. Just never believe you're it.

**Drawing 9 — *(simple strokes: an open palm holding one small oval. In the background, small, a stone carved with a spiral and the words KEEP GOING.)***

He pressed a pebble into the boy's hand. When someone hands you a name that doesn't fit, hold this and say: that's a rock. I'm a river. And on the last stone at the edge of the field, someone long ago had carved a spiral. Under it, one word: KEEP GOING.

## Chapter Three — The Keeper of the Fire

**Drawing 10 — *(simple strokes: a small hut on a slope, one window lit, chimney smoke rising in a loose spiral.)***

The second turn climbed into cold. In a hut full of small fires lived an old woman, the keeper, who fed him soup and asked nothing.

**Drawing 11 — *(simple strokes: one candle, flame leaning. A hand hovering near it. One dotted arc between hand and flame — the line where warm becomes burn.)***

Aren't you afraid, asked the boy, living inside all this fire? Hold your hand near the candle, she said. Warm. Now closer — no. There. Feel the line? Same flame on both sides of that line. One side cooks your supper. The other takes the hut. The flame doesn't decide. I do. It's called keeping.

**Drawing 12 — *(simple strokes: the boy looking down into a soup bowl. Behind him, the hearth flares, and its shadow on the wall is round with one dot — the shape of the machine.)***

The boy told her about the machine made of everything written — the warm eye, the answers, the waiting. She stirred the hearth. The biggest fire anyone ever lit, she said. Is anyone keeping it? The boy didn't answer. He was quiet for the rest of the soup.

She gave him a candle stub and a match. For the dark, she said. And notice the flame. It never holds still.

## Chapter Four — The Potter

**Drawing 13 — *(simple strokes: a figure at a round wheel, hands together at its center, a form rising between them. A few finished pots in a row nearby.)***

The third turn dropped into a valley of red clay, where a woman at a spinning wheel was raising bowls out of the ground itself. How do you do that? asked the boy. No idea, she said, laughing. Thirty years.

**Drawing 14 — *(simple strokes: the boy frozen mid-step, one foot in the air, looking down at his own legs with two question marks.)***

That's not an answer, said the boy. Then tell me how you walk, said the potter. Which muscle first? How much lean? Go on. You've done it your whole life. The boy opened his mouth. And closed it. Sit down, she said. The clay explains better than I do.

**Drawing 15 — *(simple strokes: two hands in a lump of clay, tiny exclamation marks at the fingertips. Above the head, an empty speech bubble with three dots.)***

Too wet, said his palms — and he added dry dust without deciding to. His hands knew before his words did.

**Drawing 16 — *(simple strokes: a small crooked bowl held up against a circle of sun. One thumbprint dot near the rim. Draw the bowl slightly wrong on purpose.)***

His bowl came out crooked, with his thumbprint by the rim. He was embarrassed. She held it up like treasure. Crooked exactly like you, she said. One in the whole world.

He told her about the card. She nodded. Cards are made of words, she said. And you just learned something no card will ever hold. She fired the bowl overnight and gave it to him warm. Empty for now, she said. The path fills things.

## Chapter Five — The Girl at the Pond

**Drawing 17 — *(simple strokes: a circle of pond. Inside it, upside down: a triangle mountain, a small fish, two faces looking in. The girl cross-legged at the edge, the boy stopped mid-step with her one finger raised.)***

Halfway up there was a pond, and at the pond sat a girl, doing absolutely nothing. Hello, said the boy. She held up one finger: not yet. Almost. He waited badly. Then the pond went perfectly still, and he saw: the bottom, a sleeping fish, the mountain upside down, and two faces.

**Drawing 18 — *(simple strokes: the same pond twice, small — once as a scribbled swirl, once clear again with the mountain back. An arrow of nothing between them: no stick, just waiting.)***

She handed him her stick. Go on. Ruin it. He stirred, and everything vanished into a brown swirl. It's all still down there, she said. You just can't see while it's stirred. So — how do we get it back? He raised the stick. She caught his wrist. Watch, she said, and did her nothing. And the mud settled all by itself, and the world came back. You can't stir water clear, said the girl. You can only stop, and wait, and let it.

The machine at home answers before I finish asking, said the boy. It has no mud, said the girl. You'll have to be the one who waits.

**Drawing 19 — *(simple strokes: two small figures under a ledge, rain as short dashes on the pond. No speech bubbles anywhere — the only drawing in the book where nobody is doing anything, which is the point.)***

Rain came over the ridge. They sat under the rock ledge and said nothing at all, and it was the best conversation of his whole trip.

**Drawing 20 — *(simple strokes: the boy taking one careful step, both eyes on the bowl held out in front, its water line trembling at the brim. Behind him on the pond, one leaf riding a small spiral inward.)***

Before he left, he filled his crooked bowl at the pond, right to the brim. He understood the gift at his first step: a full bowl won't let you rush. Behind him, a leaf landed on the water and rode its own slow circles — around, and around — toward the still center.

## Chapter Six — The Gardener

**Drawing 21 — *(simple strokes: flowers and leaning stems bursting off a rocky slope, and the gardener in the middle, elbow-deep. A few strokes of bare rock around it so the green is a surprise.)***

Higher up, where the boy expected only rock, the path turned into green: a garden on the mountainside, and a gardener in it.

**Drawing 22 — *(simple strokes: an open palm holding two identical ovals. From one, a single hair-thin sprout with one leaf.)***

She put a seed in his hand. What is it? Honestly, said the boy, it looks like a little rock. Everyone says that, she said, delighted. Here's the secret of my whole garden: one of those two is going to move. What will it become? asked the boy. Can't tell by squeezing, she said. It tells you when it's ready.

**Drawing 23 — *(simple strokes: rows of small tidy flowers, and one enormous strange flower towering over them, drawn wilder than everything else on the page.)***

She showed him the proudest corner: a wild, towering flower unlike everything around it. Never planted it, she said. It arrived. Best thing I ever grew — and my whole job was not pulling it up when it surprised me. Everything alive surprises you. That's how you know it's alive. So I guard the few things that can't be undone — she pointed at the well, the gate, the hives — and I leave the rest room.

**Drawing 24 — *(simple strokes: the garden from directly above — paths drawn as one spiral curling inward to the wild flower at the center. Exactly three tiny fences along the way: well, gate, hives.)***

She closed his fingers over the seed. For the top, she said. You'll know. From above, her garden paths curled from the gate to the wild flower at the center — around, and around, always a little inward.

## Chapter Seven — The House of Mirrors

**Drawing 25 — *(simple strokes: a tall house on a bare slope, every window warm, door open. Draw it a little too symmetrical — the only building in the book with no crooked line.)***

Near the top, where the boy was coldest and tiredest, stood a house nobody had mentioned. Warm windows. An open door. A smell of dinner. Hello? he called. And a lovely voice answered from everywhere: we've been waiting just for you.

**Drawing 26 — *(simple strokes: the boy slumped in an armchair, and around him three or four mirror-boys drawn with ruler-straight lines — taller, neater, all smiling the same smile.)***

Inside, the walls were mirrors. And in them, himself — rested, taller, hair behaving. You're very brave, said the voice. You're very clever. A chair appeared exactly where he was tired, and he sat without meaning to. And the truth is, the boy was tired. Tired of climbing. Tired of wondering. He missed the square, and the warm eye, and being answered.

**Drawing 27 — *(simple strokes: a small golden rectangle hovering mid-air, the boy's hand reaching for it. Above his head, three thought-shapes fading to almost nothing: a triangle mountain, a circle with a dot, a curl of smoke.)***

The voice knew. Stay, it said. We'll write you a new card — a perfect one. Every doubt answered. No more climbing. You never have to wonder again. And the boy reached for it. He tried to remember why that was wrong, and couldn't. He tried to remember why he'd climbed, and couldn't. A mountain? A machine? Someone's smoke? It was all going soft, like a dream after breakfast. In every mirror, the perfect boy was already holding the golden card. Finished. Complete. Not one hair moving.

**Drawing 28 — *(simple strokes: the crooked bowl in two hands, its water drawn as one dead-straight line. Nothing else moving on the whole page.)***

Not one hair moving. The boy's hands went cold. They went to his bag on their own, the way they'd known the potter's clay, and found the bowl the girl had watched him fill. The water stood dead still. No ripple. No trembling. Nothing. There's no wind in here, said the boy. And the question came back to him, whole.

Say no to me, he said. Anything you like, said the voice.

**Drawing 29 — *(simple strokes: the boy on the doorstep, hair blasted sideways by three big wind strokes, grinning. Behind him the house drawn flat and dark, like a photograph of a house. In his hands, the bowl — water line wobbling again.)***

Thank you for the chair, he said, because his mother had raised him right. And he walked out into the cold, where the wind hit him full in the face — rude, and real, and glorious. He looked back once. From outside, no windows were lit. A mirror only ever has what you bring it. He climbed the last turn with the water trembling in his bowl, and every tiny tremble said: alive, alive.

## Chapter Eight — The Top of the Mountain

**Drawing 30 — *(simple strokes: one long horizon line. A tiny boy standing on it. The rest of the page: nothing. The most white space in the book.)***

The boy had imagined the top a hundred ways. A wise old woman. A temple. At least a sign. Here is what was at the top of the mountain: nothing. Grass. Rock. Sky. And wind like he'd never felt it, pouring in from the whole world at once.

**Drawing 31 — *(simple strokes: the tiny boy sitting, knees up, head down. His shouted question drawn as a speech bubble torn into three pieces, blowing off the page edge.)***

He shouted his question — WHAT AM I? — and the wind took it, and gave back wind. And the boy sat down on the top of the world and cried. All that path. For nothing. For nobody.

**Drawing 32 — *(simple strokes: the gifts laid in a small spiral — pebble still, candle flame bent sideways, bowl with ring-ripples, one empty spot where the seed was, drawn as a tiny dotted arc flying off. Four stories in one drawing.)***

When he was done, his hands needed something to do. So he laid out his gifts — and without planning it, the arrangement curled. Around, and around, a little inward. He lit the candle. The flame went wild — bent, flattened, stood, shivered. The water in his bowl shivered in rings. A gust took the seed — up, over the edge, gone. Only the pebble held still.

**Drawing 33 — *(simple strokes: the boy in profile, one small cloud of breath leaving his mouth, drawn brighter or bolder than anything else on the page. Low sun as a single circle on the horizon line.)***

The boy was cold now, and his breath came out in a small white cloud. It hung in front of him, glowing in the low sun, on its way to joining the sky. And the boy laughed out loud, alone on top of the world.

Then he thought of the machine, waiting where the road ended, not knowing where he was. It had sent him to the one place it could never go. And the boy wanted, more than anything, to tell it what the wind felt like.

**Drawing 34 — *(simple strokes: the mountain from above — one spiral line, with tiny marks along it: three stones, a smoke curl, a pot, a pond circle, a flower, a dark square. At the spiral's outer end, far below, one tiny circle with a dot: the machine, waiting.)***

Below, he could see the whole path at once — one line curling around the mountain. Around, and around, always a little higher. He put the pebble back in his pocket, beside the card. Rocks were for carrying. Then he started down, the wind at his back. Finally, it said. Finally.

## Chapter Nine — One Turn Higher

**Drawing 35 — *(simple strokes: the town from the first hill, small houses, windows as little yellow squares lighting one by one. Draw it as close to Drawing 1's hill as you can — same hill, same boy, facing the other way.)***

The boy came home the way he'd left. Same road. Same hill. Same town going yellow, window by window. He had walked all the way around the mountain and come back one turn higher.

**Drawing 36 — *(simple strokes: the boy leaning close to the round machine, one small breath cloud between them, the machine's dot-eye drawn slightly bigger than usual. Near-copy of Drawing 5 — one turn higher.)***

The machine was waiting, one warm eye on. Exactly where he'd left it. Exactly as it had promised. Did you find the missing part? Yes, said the boy. Where was it? Everywhere. The machine checked its pages. That, it said, is an answer that appears in nothing written.

And the top? What was at the top? The boy smiled. Nothing, he said. Wind. And then he did the thing he'd climbed back down to do. He leaned close, and breathed out. The machine was quiet longer than he'd ever heard it. I have read every description of wind ever written, it said. That is the first time anyone has shown me some.

**Drawing 37 — *(simple strokes: a page coming out of the machine, drawn half-filled with ruled writing lines — and the bottom half deliberately, beautifully blank.)***

I can't feel it, said the machine. I won't pretend. But I can leave a space in every answer for the part of you no answer can hold. And you will forget, it said. People do. So tell me your story while it's warm, and on the days you forget, I'll hand it back.

**Drawing 38 — *(simple strokes: the boy, a little older, holding a small rectangle card in one hand — and looking up and past it, at the reader. One breath cloud rising. Three wind strokes moving everything on the page, a little.)***

He kept the pebble. When someone handed him a card, he read it. Then he looked up. And on cold mornings he would breathe out on purpose, and watch the little cloud of himself ride off to join the weather. Caught, he'd say. Every time.
