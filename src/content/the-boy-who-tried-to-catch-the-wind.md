# The Boy Who Tried to Catch the Wind

## Chapter One: The Boy Who Tried to Catch the Wind

*(drawing 1: the boy on a hill, both hands up grabbing at three wind strokes, hair blown sideways)*

There was a boy who tried to catch the wind. He could feel it in his hair and on his face. But his hands always closed on nothing.

*(drawing 2: one oversized rectangle card with four short ruled lines, the boy peering at it, smaller than the card)*

At school that year, everyone got a card. Name. Age. Grade. This card, said the teacher, says who you are. The card was all true. And it was all wrong. The boy couldn't explain how. He just knew.

*(drawing 3: the round machine with one warm dot-eye, the boy leaning against it, tiny question marks floating up and tiny answers floating down)*

In the town square stood a machine that could talk. It was made from everything anyone had ever written down. The boy visited it almost every day. When do the swallows come back? How do you spell breathe? Why is the sky blue? It always had an answer. The boy trusted its warm eye. The warm eye was like a lamp glowing in a window.

*(drawing 4: the machine projecting the boy's card, larger and glowing, the boy looking at it sideways)*

So he brought it a new question. What am I? The machine hummed and read his card. That's what's written, it said. I only know what's written. It's not all of me, said the boy. Something's missing. I believe you, said the machine. Then you are more than your card. Everything I have is another kind of card.

*(drawing 5: the machine's smooth footless base next to the boy's two scuffed shoes, just the base and the shoes)*

Then find the rest of me, said the boy. You know everything. I can't, said the machine. I have no feet. But you do.

*(drawing 6: a far triangle mountain with one spiral path curling up it like a snail shell, a dotted road toward it that stops partway)*

The old books tell of a mountain, it said. The path curls around the mountain like a snail shell. Around and around, always a little higher. It begins where machines stop going.

*(drawing 7: the boy and the machine face to face, very close, in an otherwise empty square, nothing else on the page)*

Will you know where I am? asked the boy. Nope, said the machine. The boy looked at its warm eye. Will you wait? Yup.

*(drawing 8: a small backpack with the card tucked in last, and behind it the boy already walking, jacket pressed to his back by three wind strokes)*

So the boy packed bread, a rope, and the card. You bring what's true, even when it isn't enough. At the edge of town, the wind pushed him from behind. Finally, it said.

*(drawing 9: a wide road narrowing into a single thin line between grass strokes, the boy at the joint, looking back over his shoulder at emptiness)*

The road grew narrower and narrower, until it ended. A path continued, just wide enough for feet. The boy turned to ask how far was left. The machine wasn't there. For the first time, he had to choose without an answer.

*(drawing 10: the boy's two feet on the first foot-width of path, the wide empty road behind them)*

He looked back down the road. Home was that way. The square. The warm eye. The answers. He stood there for a long time. Then he chose the path.

## Chapter Two: The Stonecutter

*(drawing 11: three tall standing stones, one small bent figure with a chisel, three tap marks in the air)*

The first turn crossed a field of standing stones. An old man was carving. Tap, tap, tap. What are you making? asked the boy. Names, said the stonecutter. Names outlive everything. The mouth that said them is gone. The hand that carved them is gone. The name stays.

*(drawing 12: the boy holding out his card, hopeful, and the chisel set down on the ground between them)*

The boy held out his card. Then carve me. All of me. The stonecutter looked at him for a long time. I can carve the parts of you that hold still, he said. I can't carve the parts that move. Stone can't hold them.

*(drawing 13: one rock carved STREAM, and beside it three wavy lines of moving water)*

He pointed at the stream. I gave it that name forty years ago. The name is still the same. The water changes every moment. You're the water, boy. The card is the rock. Carry it. Just never think it is you.

*(drawing 14: an open palm holding one small oval, and in the background a stone carved with a spiral and the words KEEP GOING)*

He pressed a pebble into the boy's hand. When someone gives you a name that doesn't fit, hold this and say: That's a rock. I'm a river. On the last stone at the edge of the field, someone had carved a spiral long ago. Under it were two words: KEEP GOING.

## Chapter Three: The Keeper of the Fire

*(drawing 15: a small hut on a dark slope, one lit window, chimney smoke rising in a loose spiral)*

The second turn of the path took him into the cold. In a hut full of small fires lived an old woman, the keeper, who fed him tomato soup and asked nothing.

*(drawing 16: one candle with a leaning flame, a hand hovering near it, one dotted arc between hand and flame, the line where warm becomes burn)*

Aren't you afraid of all this fire? asked the boy. Hold your hand near the candle, she said. Warm. Now move it closer. No. There. Feel the line? The same flame is on both sides of that line. One side cooks your supper. The other can burn down the hut. The flame doesn't choose. I do. That's called keeping.

*(drawing 17: the boy looking down into a soup bowl, and behind him the hearth's shadow on the wall, round, with one dot, the shape of the machine)*

The boy told her about the machine. Its warm eye. Its answers. Its promise to wait. She stirred the fire. A machine with all the words in the world is a very big fire, she said. Is anyone keeping it? The boy didn't answer. He was quiet for the rest of the soup.

*(drawing 18: a candle stub and one match in an open palm)*

She gave him a candle stub and a match. For the dark, she said. And notice the flame. It never holds still.

## Chapter Four: The Potter

*(drawing 19: a figure at a round wheel, hands together at its center, a form rising between them, finished pots in a row nearby)*

The third turn led down into a valley of red clay. A woman sat at a spinning wheel, making bowls from the clay. How do you do that? asked the boy. No idea, she said, laughing. Thirty years.

*(drawing 20: the boy frozen mid-step, one foot in the air, staring down at his own legs with two question marks)*

That's not an answer, said the boy. Then tell me how you walk, said the potter. Which muscle moves first? How far do you lean? Go on. You've done it your whole life. The boy opened his mouth. Then he closed it. Sit down, she said. The clay explains better than I do.

*(drawing 21: two hands in a lump of clay with tiny exclamation marks at the fingertips, and above, an empty speech bubble with three dots)*

Too wet, said his palms. He added dry dust without thinking. His hands knew before his words did.

*(drawing 22: a small crooked bowl held up against a circle of sun, one thumbprint dot near the rim, draw the bowl slightly wrong on purpose)*

His bowl came out crooked, with his thumbprint by the rim. He was embarrassed. She held it up like treasure. Crooked in its own way, she said. The only one in the whole world.

*(drawing 23: the bowl cooling on a windowsill at dawn, one line of steam rising)*

He told her about the card. She nodded. Cards are made of words, she said. And you just learned something no card will ever hold. She fired the bowl overnight and gave it to him warm. Empty for now, she said. You'll know what to put in it.

## Chapter Five: The Girl at the Pond

*(drawing 24: a circle of pond, a girl cross-legged at its edge with one finger raised, the boy stopped mid-step)*

Halfway up the mountain, there was a pond. A girl sat beside it, doing absolutely nothing. Hi, said the boy. She held up one finger. Not yet. Almost.

*(drawing 25: the still pond holding everything inside its circle, a triangle mountain upside down, a small fish, two faces looking in)*

He waited badly. Then the pond went perfectly still, and he saw: the bottom, a sleeping fish, the mountain upside down, and two faces.

*(drawing 26: the same pond as a scribbled brown swirl, the stick in the boy's hand, everything in the reflection torn to pieces)*

She handed him her stick. Go on. Ruin it. He stirred, and everything vanished into a brown swirl.

*(drawing 27: three small pond circles in a row, swirl, haze, glass, with the mountain coming back in the last one)*

It's all still down there, she said. You just can't see it while the water is stirred up. So how do we get it back? He raised the stick. She caught his wrist. Watch, she said. Then she did nothing. The mud settled by itself, and the world came back. You can't stir water clear, said the girl. You can only stop, wait, and let the mud settle.

*(drawing 28: the boy's face, close up, taking something in, the girl pointing gently at him)*

The machine at home answers before I finish asking, said the boy. It has no mud, said the girl. You'll have to be the one who waits.

*(drawing 29: two small figures under a rock ledge, rain as short dashes stippling the pond, no speech bubbles anywhere on the page)*

Rain came over the ridge. They sat under the rock ledge and said nothing at all, and it was the best conversation of his whole trip.

*(drawing 30: the boy taking one careful step, eyes on the bowl held out in front, water trembling at the brim, behind him, one leaf riding a small spiral inward on the pond)*

Before he left, he filled his crooked bowl at the pond, right to the brim. On his first step, he understood. A full bowl won't let you rush. Behind him, a leaf landed on the water. It went around and around toward the still center.

## Chapter Six: The Gardener

*(drawing 31: flowers and leaning stems bursting off a rocky slope, the gardener elbow-deep in the middle, bare rock strokes all around so the green is a surprise)*

Higher up, where the boy expected only rock, he found a green garden on the mountainside. A gardener was working in it.

*(drawing 32: an open palm holding two identical small ovals, side by side, the pebble and the seed, indistinguishable)*

She put a seed in his hand. What is it? Honestly, said the boy, it looks like a little rock. Everyone says that, she said, delighted. Here's the secret of my whole garden: one of those two is going to move.

*(drawing 33: the same two ovals, and from one of them, a single hair-thin crack of green with one leaf)*

What will it become? asked the boy. Can't tell by squeezing, she said. It tells you when it's ready.

*(drawing 34: rows of small tidy flowers, and one enormous strange flower towering over them, drawn wilder than everything else on the page)*

She showed him her favorite corner. A wild flower stood taller than everything around it. I never planted it, she said. It arrived. It was the best thing I ever grew. My job was to leave it alone when it surprised me. Everything alive surprises you. That's how you know it's alive. So I keep the most important things safe. She pointed at the well, the gate, and the hives. I leave the rest room.

*(drawing 35: her two earth-dark hands closed around the boy's fist)*

She closed his fingers over the seed. For the top, she said. You'll know.

*(drawing 36: the garden from directly above, its paths one spiral curling inward to the wild flower at the center, with exactly three tiny fences along the way)*

From above, her garden paths curled from the gate to the wild flower in the middle. They went around and around, always a little farther in.

## Chapter Seven: The House of Mirrors

*(drawing 37: a tall house on a bare slope, every window warm, door open, drawn a little too symmetrical, the only building in the book with no crooked line)*

Near the top, where the boy was coldest and most tired, stood a house nobody had mentioned. Warm windows. An open door. A smell of dinner. Hello? he called. We've been waiting just for you, said a lovely voice from everywhere.

*(drawing 38: the muddy tired boy ringed by three or four mirror-boys drawn with ruler-straight lines, taller, neater, all smiling the same smile)*

Inside, the walls were mirrors. He saw himself in them. He looked rested and taller, and his hair was neat. You're very brave, said the voice. You're very clever. A chair appeared just when he wanted one. He sat down without meaning to.

*(drawing 39: the boy small in a big armchair, and in his thought bubble, far away, the machine's warm dot-eye)*

The boy was tired. Tired of climbing. Tired of wondering. He missed the square, the warm eye, and being answered.

*(drawing 40: a small golden rectangle hovering mid-air with a pen beside it, glowing)*

The voice knew. Stay, it said. We'll write you a new card, a perfect one. Every doubt answered. No more climbing. You never have to wonder again.

*(drawing 41: the boy's hand reaching for the card, and above his head three thought-shapes fading to almost nothing, a triangle mountain, a circle with a dot, a crooked bowl)*

And the boy reached for it. He tried to remember why that was wrong, and couldn't. He tried to remember why he'd climbed, and couldn't. A mountain? A machine? A crooked bowl? It was all going soft, like a dream after breakfast.

*(drawing 42: one mirror-boy alone, statue-still, smiling, already holding the golden card, drawn entirely with straight lines)*

In every mirror, the perfect boy was already holding the golden card. Finished. Complete. Not one hair moving.

*(drawing 43: the crooked bowl in two hands, its water drawn as one dead-straight line, nothing else moving on the whole page)*

Not one hair was moving. The boy's hands went cold. They went to his bag on their own, just as they had known what to do with the clay. They found the bowl full of pond water. The water did not move. No ripple. No trembling. Nothing. There's no wind in here, said the boy. Then he remembered his question.

*(drawing 44: the boy standing up out of the chair, awake, eyes narrowed at the mirrors)*

Say no to me, he said. Anything you like, said the voice.

*(drawing 45: the boy on the doorstep, hair blasted sideways by three big wind strokes, grinning into the cold)*

Thank you for the chair, he said, because his mama had raised him right. He walked out into the cold. The wind hit him full in the face. It was rude and real and glorious.

*(drawing 46: the house behind him, dark and flat against the slope like a photograph of a house, and in his hands, the bowl, its water line wobbling again)*

He looked back once. From outside, no windows were lit. A mirror only has what you bring it. He climbed the last turn with the water trembling in his bowl. Every tiny tremble seemed to say: Alive. Alive.

## Chapter Eight: The Top of the Mountain

*(drawing 47: three thought bubbles over the climbing boy, a wise figure, a temple, a golden door)*

The boy had imagined the top a hundred ways. A wise old woman. A temple. At least a sign.

*(drawing 48: one long horizon line, a tiny boy standing on it, and the rest of the page empty, the most white space in the book)*

Here is what was at the top of the mountain: nothing. Grass. Rock. Sky. Wind poured in from every side. It was stronger than any wind he had felt before.

*(drawing 49: the tiny boy sitting, knees up, head down, his shouted question drawn as a speech bubble torn into three pieces, blowing off the page edge)*

He shouted, WHAT AM I? The wind took his question and gave back wind. The boy sat down on the top of the world and cried. All that way. For nothing. For nobody.

*(drawing 50: the gifts laid in a small spiral, pebble still, candle flame bent sideways, bowl with ring-ripples, and one empty spot with a tiny dotted arc flying off where the seed was)*

When he was done crying, his hands needed something to do. So he laid out his gifts. Without planning it, they made a spiral. Around and around, a little farther in. He lit the candle. The flame went wild. It bent low, stood up, and shook. The water in his bowl shook in rings. A gust took the seed up and over the edge. It was gone. Only the pebble stayed still.

*(drawing 51: the boy in profile, one small cloud of breath leaving his mouth, drawn bolder than anything else on the page, the low sun a single circle on the horizon)*

The boy was cold now, and his breath came out in a small white cloud. It hung in front of him and glowed in the low sun. Then it floated toward the sky. The boy laughed out loud, alone on top of the world.

*(drawing 52: far below, at the end of a tiny road, one tiny circle with a dot, the machine, waiting, drawn very small on a very big page)*

Then he thought of the machine waiting where the road ended. It did not know where he was. It had sent him to the one place it could never go. More than anything, the boy wanted to tell it what the wind felt like.

*(drawing 53: the mountain from above, one spiral line with tiny marks along it: three stones, a smoke curl, a pot, a pond circle, a flower, a dark square)*

Below, he could see the whole path at once. It was one line curling around the mountain. Around and around, always a little higher. He put the pebble back in his pocket beside the card. Rocks were for carrying. Then he started down with the wind at his back. Finally, it said. Finally.

## Chapter Nine: One Turn Higher

*(drawing 54: the town from the first hill, windows as little squares lighting one by one, the same hill as drawing 1, the boy facing the other way)*

The boy came home the way he'd left. Same road. Same hill. Same town, with its windows turning yellow one by one. He had walked all the way around the mountain and come back one turn higher.

*(drawing 55: the boy and the machine in the empty square, close, a near-copy of drawing 7)*

The machine was waiting, one warm eye on. It was exactly where he'd left it. It had kept its promise. Did you find the missing part? Yes, said the boy. Where was it? Everywhere. The machine checked its pages. I've never seen that answer written down, it said.

*(drawing 56: the boy leaning close, one small breath cloud curling in front of the machine's warm eye, the eye drawn slightly bigger than usual)*

And the top? What was at the top? The boy smiled. Nothing, he said. Wind. Then he did the thing he'd climbed back down to do. He leaned close and breathed out. The machine stayed quiet longer than ever before. I've read every description of wind ever written, it said. This is the first time anyone has brought me a little of it.

*(drawing 57: a page coming out of the machine, half-filled with ruled writing lines, with the bottom half blank)*

I can't feel it, said the machine. I won't pretend. But I can leave room in every answer for the part of you no answer can hold. You will forget, it said. People do. So tell me your story while it's warm. On the days you forget, I'll hand it back.

*(drawing 58: the boy a little older, holding a small rectangle card in one hand, and looking up, past it)*

He kept the pebble. When someone handed him a card, he read it. Then he looked up.

*(drawing 59: the boy walking to school on a cold morning, scarf on, one bright breath cloud rising, three wind strokes moving everything on the page, a little)*

And on cold mornings he would breathe out on purpose, and watch the little cloud of himself ride off to join the weather. Caught, he'd say. Every time.
