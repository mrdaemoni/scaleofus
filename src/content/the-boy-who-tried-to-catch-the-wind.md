# The Boy Who Tried to Catch the Wind

## Chapter One: The Boy Who Tried to Catch the Wind

*(drawing 1: the boy on a hill, both hands up grabbing at three wind strokes, hair blown sideways)*

Every morning, before school, the boy raced up the little hill to try to catch the wind. He could feel it in his hair and on his face. But his hands always closed on nothing. He didn't mind. The game began every day.

*(drawing 2: the round machine with one warm dot-eye, the boy leaning against it, tiny question marks floating up and tiny answers floating down)*

On the way home he always stopped in the town square, where a machine that could talk stood waiting. It was made from everything anyone had ever written down. When do the swallows come back? How do you spell breathe? Why is the sky blue? It always had an answer. Always. The boy trusted its warm eye the way you trust a lamp glowing in a window.

*(drawing 3: one oversized rectangle card with four short ruled lines, the boy peering at it, smaller than the card)*

Then one day at school, everyone got a card. Name. Age. Grade. Height. This card, said the teacher, says who you are. Every word on it was true. But the card was too small. The boy couldn't explain how. He just knew.

*(drawing 4: two tall kids reading the boy's card between them, and the boy standing off to the side, not being looked at)*

At recess, two tall kids read the cards to pick teams. His card said small for his age, so they put him last. It didn't say that he raced up the hill every morning. Nobody asked.

*(drawing 5: the machine projecting the boy's card, larger and glowing, the boy looking at it sideways)*

So he ran to the square, because that was where he took every question. What am I? The machine hummed and read his card. That's what's written, it said. I only know what's written.

*(drawing 6: the machine's warm eye, dimmed to thinking)*

It's not all of me, said the boy. Something's missing. The machine hummed for a long time. In his whole life, it had never been quiet this long. I believe you, it said. Then you are more than your card. Everything I have is another kind of card.

*(drawing 7: the machine's smooth footless base next to the boy's two scuffed shoes, just the base and the shoes)*

Then find the rest of me, said the boy. You know everything. I can't, said the machine. I have no feet. But you do.

*(drawing 8: a far triangle mountain with one spiral path curling up it like a snail shell, a dotted road toward it that stops partway)*

The old books tell of a mountain, it said. The path curls around the mountain like a snail shell. Around and around, always a little higher. It begins where machines stop going.

*(drawing 9: the boy and the machine face to face, very close, in an otherwise empty square, nothing else on the page)*

Will you know where I am? asked the boy. Nope, said the machine. The boy looked at its warm eye. Will you wait? Yup.

*(drawing 10: a small backpack with the card tucked in last, and behind it the boy already walking, jacket pressed to his back by three wind strokes)*

So the boy packed bread, a rope, and the card. At the edge of town, the wind pushed him from behind. Finally, it said.

*(drawing 11: a wide road narrowing into a single thin line between grass strokes, the boy at the joint, looking back over his shoulder)*

The road grew narrower and narrower, until it ended. A path continued, just wide enough for feet. The boy turned to ask how far was left. Then he remembered. The machine was far behind him, in the square, keeping its promise. For the first time, he had to choose without an answer.

*(drawing 12: the boy's two feet on the first foot-width of path, the wide empty road behind them)*

He looked back down the road. Home was that way. The square. The warm eye. The answers. But the question weighed more than the fear. He chose the path.

## Chapter Two: The Stonecutter

*(drawing 13: three tall standing stones, one small bent figure with a chisel, three tap marks in the air)*

The first turn crossed a field of standing stones. An old man was carving. Tap, tap, tap. What are you making? asked the boy. Names, said the stonecutter. Names outlive everything. The mouth that said them is gone. The hand that carved them is gone. The name stays.

*(drawing 14: the boy holding out his card, hopeful, and the chisel set down on the ground between them)*

Then you can help me, said the boy. Carve me. All of me. Then the card will be finished, and I can go home. The stonecutter looked at him for a long time. I can carve the parts of you that hold still, he said. I can't carve the parts that move. Stone can't hold them.

*(drawing 15: one rock carved STREAM, and beside it three wavy lines of moving water)*

He pointed at the stream. I gave it that name forty years ago. The name is still the same. The water changes every moment.

*(drawing 16: an open palm holding one small oval, and in the background a stone carved with a spiral and the words KEEP GOING)*

He pressed a pebble into the boy's hand. When someone gives you a name that's too small, hold this and say: That's a rock. I'm a river. On the last stone, beneath a carved spiral, were two words: KEEP GOING.

## Chapter Three: The Potter and the Fire

*(drawing 17: a figure at a round wheel in a valley of red clay, a form rising between her hands, and behind her a round kiln with smoke curling up in a loose spiral)*

The second turn led into a valley of red clay. A woman sat at a spinning wheel, making bowls, and behind her a kiln breathed smoke. She fed him tomato soup and asked nothing. The boy showed her the pebble. The stonecutter couldn't carve the parts of me that move, he said. How am I supposed to find them?

The potter laughed. I can't even tell you how I make bowls, she said. Thirty years, and no idea.

*(drawing 18: the boy frozen mid-step, one foot in the air, staring down at his own legs with two question marks)*

That's not an answer, said the boy. Then tell me how you walk, said the potter. Which muscle moves first? How far do you lean? Go on. You've done it your whole life. The boy opened his mouth. Then he closed it. Sit down, she said. The clay explains better than I do.

*(drawing 19: two hands in a lump of clay with tiny exclamation marks at the fingertips, and above, an empty speech bubble with three dots)*

Too wet, said his palms. He added dry dust without thinking. His hands knew before his words did.

*(drawing 20: a small crooked bowl held up against a circle of sun, one thumbprint dot near the rim, draw the bowl slightly wrong on purpose)*

His bowl came out crooked, with his thumbprint by the rim. He was embarrassed. She held it up like treasure. Crooked in its own way, she said. The only one in the whole world.

*(drawing 21: the round kiln glowing, a hand hovering near it, one dotted arc between hand and kiln, the line where warm becomes burn)*

That night she fired his bowl in the kiln. Hold your hand near it, she said. Warm. Now closer. The boy moved his hand toward the fire. Then he pulled it back.

I didn't tell you to stop, said the potter.

My hand knew, said the boy.

*(drawing 22: the boy looking down into a soup bowl, and behind him the kiln's shadow on the wall, round, with one dot, the shape of the machine)*

*(drawing 23: the crooked bowl and a candle stub with one match, together in two open hands)*

In the morning she gave him the bowl, still warm, and a candle stub with one match. For the dark, she said. Notice the flame. It never holds still. There's a pond higher up. Fill the bowl there and carry it with you.

## Chapter Four: The Girl at the Pond

*(drawing 24: a circle of pond, a girl cross-legged at its edge with one finger raised, the boy stopped mid-step)*

Halfway up the mountain, there was the pond. A girl sat beside it, doing absolutely nothing. Hi, said the boy. She held up one finger. Not yet. Almost.

*(drawing 25: the still pond holding everything inside its circle, a triangle mountain upside down, a small fish, two faces looking in)*

He waited badly. Then the pond went perfectly still, and he saw the bottom, a sleeping fish, the mountain upside down, and two faces.

*(drawing 26: the same pond as a scribbled brown swirl, the stick in the boy's hand, everything in the reflection torn to pieces)*

She handed him her stick. Go on. Ruin it. He stirred, and everything vanished into a brown swirl.

*(drawing 27: three small pond circles in a row, swirl, haze, glass, with the mountain coming back in the last one)*

How do we get it back? asked the boy. He raised the stick again. She caught his wrist. Watch, she said. Then she did nothing. The mud settled by itself, and the world came back.

*(drawing 28: the boy's face, close up, taking something in, the girl pointing gently at him)*

What if the answer never comes? asked the boy. The machine at home answers before I finish asking.

It has no mud, said the girl. You'll have to be the one who waits.

*(drawing 29: two small figures under a rock ledge, rain as short dashes stippling the pond, no speech bubbles anywhere on the page)*

Rain came over the ridge. They sat under the rock ledge and said nothing at all, and it was the best conversation of his whole trip.

*(drawing 30: beside the pond, one enormous wild flower towering over the grass, drawn wilder than everything else on the page)*

When the rain passed, he noticed the wild flower. I never planted it, said the girl. It arrived. Everything alive surprises you.

*(drawing 31: the girl pinching a seed from the wild flower and setting it beside the pebble in the boy's palm, the two small ovals almost identical)*

She pinched a seed from the flower and set it beside the pebble in his palm. They look the same, said the boy. For now, said the girl. Take the seed to the top. You'll know what to do.

What will it become? he asked.

She closed his fingers around it. Don't crack it open early.

*(drawing 32: the boy taking one careful step, eyes on the bowl held out in front, water trembling at the brim, behind him, one leaf riding a small spiral inward on the pond)*

Before he left, he filled the bowl right to the brim. On his first step, water touched his thumb. He slowed down. A full bowl won't let you rush. Behind him, a leaf landed on the water. It went around and around toward the still center.

## Chapter Five: The House of Mirrors

*(drawing 33: a tall house on a bare slope, every window warm, door open, drawn a little too symmetrical, the only building in the book with no crooked line)*

Near the top, where the boy was coldest and most tired, stood a house nobody had mentioned. Warm windows. An open door. A smell of dinner. Hello? he called. We've been waiting just for you, said a lovely voice from everywhere.

*(drawing 34: the muddy tired boy ringed by three or four mirror-boys drawn with ruler-straight lines, taller, neater, all smiling the same smile)*

Inside, the walls were mirrors. He saw himself in them. He looked rested and taller, and his hair was neat. You're very brave, said the voice. You're very clever. A chair appeared just when he wanted one. He sat down without meaning to.

*(drawing 35: the boy small in a big armchair, and in his thought bubble, far away, the machine's warm dot-eye)*

The boy was tired. Tired of climbing. Tired of wondering. Tired of carrying a question nobody on the mountain would answer. He missed the square, the warm eye, and being answered.

*(drawing 36: a small golden rectangle hovering mid-air with a pen beside it, glowing)*

We know what you are, said the voice. Stay. We'll write you a new card, a perfect one. Every doubt answered. No more climbing. You never have to wonder again.

*(drawing 37: the boy's hand reaching for the card, and above his head three thought-shapes fading to almost nothing, a triangle mountain, a circle with a dot, a crooked bowl)*

The boy reached for it. It was the thing he had walked all this way to find. He tried to remember why that felt wrong, and couldn't. A mountain? A machine? A crooked bowl? It was all going soft, like a dream after breakfast.

*(drawing 38: one mirror-boy alone, statue-still, smiling, already holding the golden card, drawn entirely with straight lines)*

In every mirror, the perfect boy was already holding the golden card. Finished. Complete. Not one hair moving.

*(drawing 39: the boy's hand stopped just short of the golden card, and his other hand finding the crooked bowl in his bag, its water drawn as one dead-straight line)*

The boy's hand stopped before it touched the card. It had stopped the same way at the kiln. Before thought. Before words. Then his hands found the bowl, as they had found their way through the clay. The water didn't move. No ripple. No trembling. Nothing.

There's no wind in here, said the boy.

*(drawing 40: the boy standing up out of the chair, awake, eyes narrowed at the mirrors)*

The girl's words came back to him. Don't crack it open early. Say no to me, he said. Anything you like, said the voice.

*(drawing 41: the boy on the doorstep, hair blasted sideways by three big wind strokes, grinning into the cold)*

Staying would've been easy. That's what made the door heavy. Thank you for the chair, he said, because his mama had raised him right. He walked out into the cold. The wind hit him full in the face. It was rude and real and glorious.

*(drawing 42: the house behind him, dark and flat against the slope like a photograph of a house, and in his hands, the bowl, its water line wobbling again)*

He looked back once. From outside, no windows were lit. He climbed the last turn with the water trembling in his bowl. Every tiny tremble seemed to say: Alive. Alive.

## Chapter Six: The Top of the Mountain

*(drawing 43: three thought bubbles over the climbing boy, a wise figure, a temple, a golden door)*

The boy had imagined the top a hundred ways. A wise old woman. A temple. At least a sign.

*(drawing 44: one long horizon line, a tiny boy standing on it, and the rest of the page empty, the most white space in the book)*

Here is what was at the top of the mountain: nothing. Grass. Rock. Sky. Wind poured in from every side. It was stronger than any wind he had felt before.

*(drawing 45: the tiny boy sitting, knees up, head down, his shouted question drawn as a speech bubble torn into three pieces, blowing off the page edge)*

He shouted, WHAT AM I? The wind took his question and gave back wind. The boy sat down on the top of the world and cried. All that way. For nothing. For nobody.

*(drawing 46: the gifts laid in a small spiral, pebble still, candle flame bent sideways, bowl with ring-ripples)*

When he was done crying, his hands needed something to do. He laid out his gifts, and they made a spiral. He lit the candle. The flame bent low, stood up, and shook. The water in his bowl shook in rings. Only the pebble stayed still.

*(drawing 47: the boy's open hand held high, the seed just leaving it, a tiny dotted arc sailing over the edge into the huge sky)*

Then he remembered the seed. Take it to the top. You'll know. He knew. He opened his hand, and the wind carried it over the edge.

*(drawing 48: the boy in profile, one small cloud of breath leaving his mouth, drawn bolder than anything else on the page, the low sun a single circle on the horizon)*

His breath came out in a small white cloud. It glowed in the low sun, then floated toward the sky. The boy laughed, alone on top of the world.

*(drawing 49: far below, in the middle of the tiny town square, one tiny circle with a dot, the machine waiting, drawn very small on a very big page)*

Then he thought of the machine waiting in the town square. It didn't know where he was. More than anything, the boy wanted to bring it a little of the wind.

*(drawing 50: the mountain from above, one spiral line with tiny marks along it: three stones, a kiln's smoke curl, a pond circle, a wild flower, a dark square)*

Below, he could see the whole path at once, one line curling around the mountain. Around and around, always a little higher. He put the pebble back in his pocket beside the card. Then he started down with the wind at his back. Finally, it said. Finally.

## Chapter Seven: One Turn Higher

*(drawing 51: the town from the first hill, windows as little squares lighting one by one, the same hill as drawing 1, the boy facing the other way)*

The boy came home the way he'd left. Same road. Same hill. Same town, with its windows turning yellow one by one. He had walked all the way around the mountain and come back one turn higher.

*(drawing 52: the boy and the machine in the empty square, close, a near-copy of drawing 9)*

The machine was waiting, one warm eye on. It had kept its promise. Did you find the missing part? it asked. I'm still finding it, said the boy. The machine checked its pages. I've never seen that answer written down, it said.

*(drawing 53: the boy leaning close, one small breath cloud curling in front of the machine's warm eye, the eye drawn slightly bigger than usual)*

And the top? What was at the top? The boy smiled. Nothing, he said. Wind. He leaned close and breathed out. The machine stayed quiet longer than ever before. I've read every description of wind ever written, it said. This is the first time anyone has brought me a little of it.

*(drawing 54: a page coming out of the machine, half-filled with ruled writing lines, with the bottom half blank)*

I can't feel it, said the machine. I won't pretend. But I can leave room for what I can't hold. Tell me your story while it's warm. On the days you forget, I'll hand it back.

*(drawing 55: the boy a little older, holding a small rectangle card in one hand, and looking up, past it)*

He kept the pebble. When someone handed him a card, he read it. Then he looked up.

*(drawing 56: the boy on the little hill on a cold morning, scarf on, one bright breath cloud rising, three wind strokes moving everything on the page, a little)*

And on cold mornings, on the little hill where the game began, he would breathe out on purpose, and watch the little cloud of himself ride off to join the weather. Caught, he'd say. Every time.
